Foundational Models & Theory

The Three Overlapping Models

Lea and Street identified three conceptual models that guide understanding of student writing and literacy in academic contexts. The first, the study skills model, views literacy as an individual, cognitive skill, focusing on surface language features and presuming easy transferability across contexts. The second, the academic socialization model, concerns students' acculturation into disciplinary discourses, assuming these genres are relatively stable and can be reproduced once learned.

These models, while influential, often frame student difficulties as deficits. The skills model treats differences as lack of technique, while the socialization model can imply a passive absorption of norms. This sets the stage for the third, more nuanced model that challenges these deficit perspectives by situating literacy within broader social and institutional dynamics, moving beyond simple skill acquisition or socialisation.

Rather than engaging in debates about good or bad writing, the academic literacies model conceptualizes writing at the level of epistemology.

The Academic Literacies Model

The academic literacies model is the core framework proposed by Lea and Street. It treats reading and writing as social practices that vary with context, culture, and genre, not as neutral or transferable skills. This model foregrounds meaning-making, identity, power, and authority, viewing student writing and learning as issues of epistemology and identity. It examines how literacy practices are shaped by and shape institutional requirements, disciplinary norms, and social processes, including power relations.

Unlike the socialization model, it sees the acquisition of academic literacies as complex, dynamic, and situated. It acknowledges that students must navigate not just disciplinary discourses, but also broader institutional discourses (like plagiarism policies or feedback mechanisms). This model emphasizes the active role of students in negotiating and constructing meaning within these varied and sometimes conflicting demands, highlighting the contested nature of academic knowledge.

The academic literacies model goes further by focusing on the relationship of epistemology and writing... in institutional requirements, as well as in more specific contexts.

Application & Practice

Navigating Genres and Modes

A key feature of student experience, highlighted by the academic literacies model, is the constant navigation and switching between different genres and modes of communication. Genres are types of text (discussions, notes, essays) with specific expectations, while modes refer to channels like speech, writing, or visual layout. Students must shift from internal thoughts to group talk, from spoken discussion to written notes, and from notes to formal essays, each requiring different semiotic resources and linguistic features.

This complex navigation demonstrates why a narrow focus on technical skills is inadequate. The model emphasizes the need for explicit attention to these shifts and transformations. Understanding that writing involves not just words but also layout, and that meaning is constructed through the interplay of multiple modes, is crucial. Students often struggle because they are not made explicitly aware of the distinct qualities and requirements of each genre and mode they encounter across different academic settings.

Students must develop awareness of the different qualities associated with various genres and modes as they move between communication contexts in academic work.

Pedagogy & Design Frame

Lea and Street argue the academic literacies model should function as a 'design frame' for pedagogy, curriculum, and instruction. This means making explicit the often hidden demands of genre and mode switching, and helping students understand the social and epistemological nature of academic tasks. It involves collaborative investigation by educators and students into the range of practices, identities, and meaning-making processes involved, rather than treating students as passive recipients or deficient.

Examples like the Academic Literacy Development Programme and the Open University Law course illustrate this. Teaching focused explicitly on the shifts between thoughts, talk, notes, presentations, and essays, helping students understand the distinct semiotic demands. For educators, it means reflecting on their own writing practices and identities, recognizing the complexity of constructing academic knowledge through specific, contextualized writing practices, not just transmitting content.

Treating such students as collaborators in the development of the academic literacies necessary... can perhaps offer a different and more supportive route to 'Widening Participation'.

Implications & Broader Context

The academic literacies model has broad implications for understanding education across contexts, from K-12 to higher education. It challenges dominant deficit models by highlighting the specificity and variety of institutional practices students encounter. By foregrounding issues of power, identity, and meaning-making, it moves beyond simplistic views of literacy as skill or socialization, linking directly to broader sociological and critical theories about knowledge and education.

This perspective encourages educators to view student struggles not as individual failings, but as challenges inherent in navigating complex, often contested, academic landscapes. It emphasizes the importance of making explicit the social nature of academic knowledge and the diverse literacy practices required for participation. This understanding supports more inclusive and reflective pedagogical approaches that recognize and build upon students' existing repertoires and experiences.

Approaches to student writing and literacy in academic contexts could be conceptualized through the use of three overlapping perspectives or models.
