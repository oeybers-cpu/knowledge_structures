On Literacy and the Social Organisation of Knowledge

Core Insight

Bernstein's concepts of vertical and horizontal knowledge structures offer a powerful lens for understanding how academic disciplines organise knowledge, which directly informs the academic literacies model's focus on the social practices of reading and writing.

Bernstein's Core Concepts

Vertical Knowledge Structures

Vertical knowledge structures are characterised by strong classification and strong framing. This means there are clear boundaries between knowledge areas (strong classification) and explicit, hierarchical rules for how knowledge is acquired and validated (strong framing). Knowledge is cumulative, building upon itself in a coherent, integrated way.

Applying this to academic literacies, vertical structures correspond to disciplines where writing practices are highly codified and cumulative. Students must master specific genres and conventions that build upon each other. The academic literacies model helps reveal how explicit rules shape the literacy practices students must learn.

In vertical structures, knowledge is cumulative, building upon itself in a coherent, integrated way.

Horizontal Knowledge Structures

Horizontal knowledge structures feature weak classification and weak framing. Boundaries between knowledge areas are less defined (weak classification), and rules for acquiring and validating knowledge are more context-dependent and implicit (weak framing). Knowledge areas exist side-by-side, often incommensurable with each other.

The academic literacies model is particularly valuable for understanding horizontal structures. It highlights how students must navigate diverse, often competing genres and discourses within these fields. The model emphasises the situated nature of literacy practices, where meaning, identity, and power are negotiated differently in each segment.

In horizontal structures, knowledge areas exist side-by-side, often incommensurable with each other.

Connecting the Frameworks

Bernstein's framework helps us understand the complexity of student experience in navigating academic literacies. The requirement to switch between genres and modes reflects the underlying structure of the knowledge domain.

In vertical structures, genre shifts often follow predictable, hierarchical patterns. In horizontal structures, they require constant negotiation and adaptation. The academic literacies model emphasises that these shifts are not merely technical. Each genre and mode embodies different social meanings, identities, and power relations. Students must learn not just the forms, but the social practices embedded within them.

Students must develop awareness of the different qualities associated with various genres and modes as they move between communication contexts in academic work.

Pedagogical Applications

Bernstein's concepts are crucial for curriculum design and pedagogy. Recognising whether a subject area is predominantly vertical or horizontal helps educators anticipate the literacy challenges students will face.

Lea and Street's academic literacies model operationalises this by advocating for a 'design frame' that makes the social and epistemological nature of academic tasks explicit. This moves beyond deficit models to a more nuanced understanding of student learning. Rather than viewing students as lacking skills, educators can see them as navigating complex, socially situated literacy practices.

Treating such students as collaborators in the development of the academic literacies necessary... can perhaps offer a different and more supportive route.
